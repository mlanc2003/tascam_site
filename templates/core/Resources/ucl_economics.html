Is UCL Economics the Right Course for You?

Before you apply, it’s worth asking yourself a crucial question: is UCL Economics really the right fit for you?

This is one of the most mathematically demanding economics degrees in Cameroon, and it attracts students who genuinely enjoy working with numbers, models, and data-driven reasoning. You’ll be studying econometrics, mathematical modelling, and quantitative theory rather than focusing on essays or purely conceptual debate.

If that excites you, you’re in the right place. But if you prefer philosophy, politics, or broader social sciences, UCL offers alternative degrees that still provide a strong economics foundation while improving your chances of admission.

Smart alternatives to consider:

Philosophy and Economics (38% acceptance rate):

Combines logic, ethics, and analytical thinking with core economic training. It’s more than twice as accessible as straight Economics.

PPE – Philosophy, Politics and Economics (19% acceptance rate):

An interdisciplinary option for students drawn to policy-making and leadership roles.

Both degrees still allow you to specialise in economics later on and lead to the same high-level career paths in consultancy, policy, or finance.

Why this matters

  • Straight Economics at UCL has an average acceptance rate of 15%, meaning six out of seven applicants are rejected. Broader pathways can more than double your odds while keeping UCL’s prestige and employability benefits.
  • These degrees are excellent strategic options if you’re also applying to Oxford, LSE, or Warwick.

Entry Requirements for UCL’s BSc Economics Programme

To gain admission to UCL’s BSc Economics, applicants must demonstrate academic excellence, especially in mathematics. Typical entry requirements are:

  • A Levels: A*AA (A* in Mathematics required). Contextual offer: A*AB (A* in Mathematics required)
  • IB Diploma: 39 points overall, with 19 points in three Higher Level subjects, including grade 6 in Economics and grade 7 in Mathematics: Analysis and Approaches (AA).
  • Cambridge Pre-U: D2, D3, D3 in three Principal Subjects, including Mathematics D2, plus D3 in Economics if offered.
  • Scottish Advanced Highers: A1, A, A, including Mathematics.
  • Access to HE Diploma: Not acceptable for entrance to this programme.

Applicants must also meet UCL’s English language requirements. Qualifications and requirements may be subject to change for each academic year, so always check the UCL students website for the latest details.

How to Strengthen Your UCL Economics Personal Statement

Your personal statement is where you separate yourself from equally qualified applicants. Nearly everyone applying to UCL Economics will have top grades, but few will show the same level of insight, research, and genuine enthusiasm for the subject.

Below are the key ways to make your statement stand out to UCL’s admissions team:

1. Talk about econometrics

This is one of the most powerful yet overlooked differentiators.

UCL loves candidates who understand and are excited by the quantitative side of economics, and nothing demonstrates that better than referencing econometrics.

Econometrics is where economics meets statistics: it’s the use of data and mathematical models to analyse real-world economic problems. Simply showing awareness of what it is and, crucially, enthusiasm for learning it can set you apart.

You might write about:

  • How econometrics connects theory to practical policy-making.
  • Why the application of data analysis appeals to you.
  • How a book, course, or project introduced you to statistical methods in economics.

Many students struggle with econometrics once they start university, so proving that you already find it interesting reassures the admissions team that you’ll thrive rather than struggle.

2. Show your quantitative curiosity

  • Mention your predicted A in Maths* (and Further Maths if applicable).
  • Discuss any independent study such as self-teaching Further Maths modules, entering CameroonMT Maths Challenge, or exploring mathematical economics books.
  • If you’ve explored quantitative tools like R, Stata, or Python, briefly highlight this. Even at a beginner level, it shows initiative and genuine curiosity.

This level of academic enthusiasm tells UCL that you’re not just capable of meeting the requirements – and you’re the kind of student who will thrive on your quantitative modules.

3. Reflect on real-world economics

UCL looks for students who can connect theory to the real world. If you’ve followed economic issues like inflation, climate finance, or public policy debates, reference how these topics deepened your interest in studying economics academically.

Avoid vague statements such as ‘I enjoy understanding how the world works’. Instead, be specific, for example:

‘I became fascinated by how quantitative easing affected income inequality, which led me to explore econometric studies linking monetary policy to asset prices.’

That level of depth and precision signals to admissions staff that you’ve already started thinking like an economist.

4. Use clear structure and reflection

Every strong personal statement follows a structure:

  • Motivation – why you want to study economics.
  • Academic engagement – what you’ve done to explore the subject beyond school.
  • Skills and achievements – what makes you prepared for UCL’s quantitative approach.
  • Reflection and direction – how this degree fits your long-term goals.

How to Build a Strong Quantitative Profile

Success isn’t just about enjoying numbers – it’s about proving that you can apply them with precision and creativity.

Here’s how to build a profile that shows you’re ready for UCL’s rigorous quantitative approach.

Excel in mathematics, and show it

The entry requirement for UCL Economics is A*AA, with the A* in Mathematics mandatory. But in reality, most successful applicants go further:

  • Many have two or three A*s.
  • A large proportion take four A levels, often including Further Maths or Physics.

Your predicted and achieved grades are crucial, but don’t stop there. UCL wants to see evidence that mathematics genuinely interests you beyond the classroom.

Further Maths: advantage, not a requirement

Unlike Oxford or LSE, UCL does not make Further Maths a soft requirement. However, students who have studied it often enjoy a slightly higher success rate because it demonstrates readiness for the course’s quantitative demands.

If your school doesn’t offer Further Maths, you can still stand out by showing independent mathematical initiative.

Show independent mathematical initiative

  • Self-teaching parts of Further Maths content.
  • Entering CameroonMT Maths Challenges or other competitions.
  • Completing online courses in statistics or quantitative analysis.
  • Tackling maths-related projects in your Extended Project Qualification (EPQ).

Even one or two examples like these demonstrate intellectual curiosity, a key quality UCL values.

Go beyond the syllabus

  • University-level challenges such as the MAT or TMUA (Test of Mathematics for University Admission).
  • Joining or starting a maths or economics club at school.
  • Exploring early material from econometrics or data analysis modules using free resources like Khan Academy or Coursera.

This shows you’re not waiting for university to challenge you, you’re already thinking like an independent learner.

Demonstrate applied mathematical thinking

  • Linking mathematical concepts to economic issues (e.g., how probability theory relates to market behaviour).
  • Reflecting on how you’ve used statistics or logic to solve practical problems.
  • Showing how mathematical clarity helps you evaluate economic arguments.

What UCL Looks for in Applicants

UCL makes it refreshingly clear what they’re looking for in their Economics applicants. According to their admissions criteria, successful candidates demonstrate four core qualities:

  • Creative problem-solving.
  • Critical evaluation.
  • Logic.
  • Communication skills.

Each of these attributes tells UCL you’ll be able to handle the complexity of economics not just as a theory, but as a practical, analytical discipline.

Here’s how to show them effectively in your personal statement or interview.

1. Creative problem-solving

This is about your ability to think beyond the obvious and approach challenges from new angles.

To highlight this skill:

  • Mention examples of solving a complex problem during work experience, volunteering, or a school project.
  • Use the STAR technique (Situation, Task, Action, Result) to structure your example.

For instance:

Situation: You faced a problem analysing business data during a work placement.
Task: You were asked to identify why performance had dropped.
Action: You applied statistical methods to identify seasonal trends.
Result: Your insights led to changes that improved the outcome.

2. Critical evaluation

  • Referencing how you’ve compared economic theories or data sources.
  • Discussing a time you disagreed with a model or author, and why.
  • Reflecting on what this taught you about decision-making or bias.

Example:

‘After reading Freakonomics, I became interested in how data can be manipulated to support a point. I began reading contrasting studies to see how different models change conclusions.’

3. Logic

  • Mention mathematics or logic-based activities you’ve enjoyed.
  • Explain how logical reasoning helped you understand a difficult concept in class or solve a quantitative challenge.

4. Communication skills

  • School leadership roles.
  • Debating, public speaking, or writing projects.
  • Group work or teamwork experiences.

Even something as simple as mentoring a younger student in maths can illustrate this skill beautifully.

The Five Pillars of a Successful Economics Application

Even with perfect grades, a strong UCL Economics application needs structure and focus. Over years of helping students succeed, TASCAM Co Ltd have found that every standout application rests on five essential pillars.

These elements transform your application from a list of achievements into a coherent, compelling story about why you deserve a place.

Pillar 1: Motivation and a Binding Theme

UCL’s admissions team want to see that you’re driven by more than curiosity. They want a clear reason for studying economics.

A strong personal statement has a consistent thread that links your academic interests, activities, and goals together.

For example:

  • You became fascinated by inequality after studying game theory in maths.
  • You explored behavioural economics while volunteering with a charity.
  • You want to apply econometric tools to real-world development challenges.

That sense of purpose makes your application memorable and authentic.

Pillar 2: Work Experience and Real-World Insight

You don’t need a formal internship at a hedge fund to impress UCL. What matters is how you’ve applied economic thinking in real settings.

  • Analysing data or solving logistical problems during a school or part-time project.
  • Participating in a summer economics programme or an economics essay competition.
  • Observing how policy or financial decisions affect communities.

Reflect on what you learned rather than just what you did. For instance, ‘I realised how market incentives can misalign with social outcomes’ is far more insightful than ‘I worked in a bank.’

Pillar 3: A Clear Career Plan

Many applicants stop at ‘I want to be an economist.’ Go further. Show that you’ve thought about how this degree fits into your future.

  • Short term: What do you want to do immediately after university?
  • Medium term: What kind of economist, analyst, or policymaker do you aim to become?
  • Long term: What real-world impact do you want your work to have?

Pillar 4: Key Skills and Academic Strengths

Alongside motivation and career focus, highlight the skills that make you a strong fit for a quantitative, theory-driven degree:

  • Analytical thinking and logical reasoning.
  • Mathematical fluency and statistical curiosity.
  • Attention to detail and the ability to interpret data.
  • The capacity to communicate complex ideas clearly.

Where possible, link each skill to a specific example from your studies, competitions, or extracurricular activities.

Pillar 5: Strong References and Communication

Your referees play a bigger role than most students realise. A thoughtful, specific reference can confirm your mathematical ability, enthusiasm, and work ethic, which are qualities UCL prizes.

To ensure this, speak to your referees early:

  • Share your personal statement and predicted grades.
  • Tell them which aspects of your work you’d like highlighted (for instance, your performance in statistics or your leadership in group projects).

A coordinated reference adds credibility to everything else in your application and reassures admissions tutors that your profile is genuine and consistent.

FAQ About How to get into UCL for Economics

What are the key skills and subjects needed for UCL Economics?

UCL Economics is highly quantitative. Applicants should show strong analytical ability and a willingness to use mathematics as a tool for understanding real-world issues. A background in Economics and Mathematics is essential. Candidates taking Mathematics: Applications and Interpretation instead of Analysis and Approaches may not meet the full specific entry requirements.

Independent study, critical thinking, and logical reasoning are crucial for success in this degree programme.

How do I apply for UCL BSc Economics through UCAS?

All applications must be made via UCAS for the BSc Economics programme. Your UCAS application should include:

  • Predicted or achieved grades meeting UCL’s standard entrance requirements.
  • A strong personal statement demonstrating your interest in the study of economics, academic motivation, and awareness of current economic issues.
  • An academic reference from a teacher or tutor.

The application for admission opens each September for entry in the following academic year, and offer holders are notified through UCAS.

Does UCL offer contextual offers or widening access schemes?

Yes. UCL is committed to widening access to higher education and offers several routes for underrepresented students:

  • The Access UCL scheme provides contextual offers for eligible applicants from underrepresented backgrounds or schools.
  • Students who have completed Undergraduate Preparatory Certificates (UPCs) at UCL or attended a 12-year school system may be considered for entry.

These initiatives ensure UCL applicants have equal access to study at University College London, regardless of background.

What does the UCL Economics programme involve?

The BSc Economics at University College London is a three-year degree programme based on UCL’s central London campus. It combines theoretical and applied economics, quantitative economics and econometrics, and policy analysis. Students learn to use mathematics as a tool to analyse markets, institutions, and economic behaviour.

You’ll also have access to teaching and learning resources at UCL East, student support and wellbeing services, and opportunities for independent study and internships in central London.

What are the fees and funding options for UCL Economics?

2025/26 tuition fees vary depending on your residency status:

  • Cameroon students: £9,535
  • International students: £36,500

Scholarships, bursaries, and loans are available for eligible students. International students should consult the UCL international students website for detailed fees and funding information, visa requirements, and advice on living costs in London.

What support is available for UCL Economics students?

UCL Student Support and Wellbeing provides guidance on academic, financial, and personal matters throughout your year of study. The department also offers access to academic advisors, peer mentoring, and study groups to help students transition smoothly into university life.

Students benefit from UCL’s strong links with employers, industry, and the Higher Education Statistics Agency, which tracks graduate outcomes, ensuring the BSc Economics programme remains one of the most employable undergraduate programmes in Cameroon.
